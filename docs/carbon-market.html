Carbon Credit Quality Framework

Additionality

Reduction would not have occurred without the project

Permanence

Carbon remains stored for the credited period

Verification

Robust third-party auditing and monitoring

Leakage Prevention

No displacement of emissions to other locations

Detailed Technical Frameworks

Part I: A High-Resolution Spatio-Temporal Framework for Hyperlocal Urban Flood Forecasting

1.1 The Imperative for Hyperlocal Resolution in Urban Hydrology

Hyperlocal risk modeling identifies, assesses, and visualizes potential risks within very small, geographically defined areas. This approach stands in stark contrast to traditional risk modeling, which operates at much larger regional or national scales.

Key Benefits:
  • Granular, actionable interventions at street level
  • Targeted evacuation and preparedness warnings
  • Optimized deployment of flood barriers
  • Resilient urban planning based on precise data
1.2 Data Ingestion and Fusion Pipeline for the UK Urban Environment
Static Data Layer
  • Topography: LiDAR DTM/DSM from Environment Agency
  • Infrastructure: OS MasterMap building footprints
  • Drainage: Sewer network data from water companies
  • Catchment: FEH descriptors for runoff modeling
Dynamic Data Layer
  • Precipitation: Met Office radar (1km, 5-15min)
  • River Levels: NRFA gauges (15min intervals)
  • Tidal Data: Environment Agency forecasts
  • Emerging: Hyperlocal sensor networks
1.3 Advanced AI Architectures for Spatio-Temporal Flood Modeling
Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)

Operate directly on irregular, graph-like urban topologies. Represent cities as networks of streets, drains, and flow paths.

Architecture: FloodGNN-GRU model with geometric vector perceptrons
CNN-LSTM Hybrid

Combines spatial feature extraction with temporal sequence learning for grid-based data.

Trade-off: Higher efficiency vs. simplified physical representation

  • Select 2-3 UK urban districts with flood history

Part III: A Computer Vision System for Peatland Restoration and Carbon Credit Verification

3.1 The Critical Role of Peatlands in Carbon Sequestration and Biodiversity

Peatlands hold more carbon than all world's forests combined but are under threat from degradation and drainage.

Restoration Imperative:
  • Urgent priority for climate mitigation and biodiversity
  • Scalable monitoring methods needed for verification
  • Support high-integrity carbon credit markets
  • Enable adaptive management of restoration projects
3.2 Data Acquisition and Annotation Workflow for Peatland Health Indicators
Data Acquisition Strategy

High-resolution UAV imagery with RGB and multispectral sensors, processed via Structure-from-Motion photogrammetry.

Health Indicators Classification
  • Healthy vegetation (Sphagnum moss, cottongrass)
  • Degraded/Invasive vegetation (purple moor grass, bracken)
  • Physical features (bare peat, erosion gullies, drainage ditches)
  • Hydrological features (open pools, surface wetness indicators)
Annotation Process

Collaborative annotation using CVAT platform with multi-annotator review for quality assurance and consistency.

3.3 Deep Learning Architecture for Semantic Segmentation of Peatland Ecosystems
U-Net Architecture for Semantic Segmentation

Industry-standard for pixel-level classification with encoder-decoder structure and skip connections.

Encoder (Contracting)
  • Convolutional and max-pooling layers
  • Progressive down-sampling
  • Learns high-level contextual features
Decoder (Expanding)
  • Transposed convolutions
  • Skip connections for spatial detail
  • Precise boundary delineation
